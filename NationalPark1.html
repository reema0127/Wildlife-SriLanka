Explore the WildLife of Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka boasts a rich tapestry of wildlife, encompassing both its diverse flora and fauna thriving in their natural habitats. Remarkably, the country exhibits one of the highest rates of biological endemism, with 16% of its fauna and 23% of flowering plants being exclusive to the region. The most recent comprehensive update on Sri Lanka's biodiversity was presented in the 6th National Report of the Convention on Biological Diversity. This report, concluding at the end of 2018, offered an up-to-date inventory of species.

However, subsequent to this report, 54 new species were meticulously documented in 2019. This impressive tally includes 26 spiders (comprising 14 cellar spiders, 7 jumping spiders, 4 crab spiders, and 1 tarantula), 1 scorpion species, 5 mites and ticks, 14 reptiles (13 day geckos and 1 snake), 1 shrub frog, 1 orchid, and 6 lichens. In a subsequent update in 2021, Professors Devaka Weerakoon and Amila Sumanapala released a revised list of taxa, incorporating data up to the conclusion of 2020.

Sri Lanka's wildlife is exceptionally diverse, housing approximately 123 species of mammals, 185 species of reptiles, 122 species of amphibians, 227 species of birds, and 93 species of freshwater fish. The constant discovery and documentation of new species underscore the ongoing vibrancy and significance of Sri Lanka's unique and thriving ecosystems.

Wild Life Sites in Sri Lanka

  • Sinharaja Forest Reserve

    • The Sinharaja Forest Reserve stands as a crucial biodiversity hotspot in Sri Lanka, holding international significance as evidenced by its designation as a Biosphere Reserve and World Heritage Site by UNESCO. Recognized by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), Sinharaja represents the nation's last surviving expanse of primary tropical rainforest. Over 60% of its trees are endemic, with many classified as rare species. The reserve harbors half of Sri Lanka's endemic animal species, encompassing butterflies, amphibians, birds, snakes, and fish. Notably, it serves as a haven for 95% of the country's endemic bird population.

    Situated within the Sri Lanka lowland rain forests ecoregion, the undulating virgin rainforest was safeguarded from extensive commercial logging due to its challenging accessibility. Consequently, it earned the distinction of being a World Biosphere Reserve in 1978 and a World Heritage Site in 1988.

    The dense vegetation of Sinharaja poses challenges for wildlife observation compared to drier national parks like Yala. The most prevalent larger mammal within its confines is the endemic purple-faced langur. Avian life thrives in mixed feeding flocks, often led by the fearless Sri Lanka crested drongo and the vocal orange-billed babbler. Among the 26 endemic bird species of Sri Lanka, all 20 rainforest species find their habitat here, featuring elusive species such as the red-faced malkoha, green-billed coucal, and Sri Lanka blue magpie.

    The reptile population boasts endemic species like the green pit viper and hump-nosed vipers, while a diverse array of amphibians, particularly tree frogs, contribute to the rich biodiversity. Among the invertebrates, notable residents include the endemic Sri Lankan birdwing butterfly and leeches

Leopard
  • Similar to many feline species, this cat demonstrates practicality in its dietary preferences, encompassing small mammals, birds, reptiles, and larger animals. In the dry zone, the Sri Lankan axis deer constitute a significant portion of its diet.
  • Additionally, the animal preys on sambar, barking deer, wild boar, and monkeys.
  • Sri Lankan leopards are generally medium-sized big cats.
  • Adult males typically weigh between 60 to 90 kg (132 to 198 lbs), while females are slightly smaller, weighing around 30 to 60 kg (66 to 132 lbs).
  • The coat of the Sri Lankan leopard is golden-yellow with a rosette pattern of black spots and circular shapes.
  • The rosettes may be smaller than those found on other leopard subspecies.
  • The Sri Lankan leopard is a subspecies of the leopard that is native to Sri Lanka, making it endemic to the island.
  • The population of Sri Lankan leopards in the wild is now fewer than 1000 individuals.
    Indian elephant
    • They are herbivores with a primarily vegetarian diet.
    • Their diet consists of a variety of plant materials, and they consume a large quantity of food due to their size.
    • Here are some key components of their diet:
    1. Grasses and Forage
    2. Leaves
    3. Bark
    4. Fruits
    5. Vegetation in Wetlands
    • They are among the largest land animals. Adult males are generally larger than females. Males can stand around 2.7 to 3.5 meters (9 to 11.5 feet) at the shoulder, while females are slightly smaller.
    • They have a robust and sturdy build. The body is characterized by a high, arched back.
    • The trunk of an Indian elephant is long, muscular, and versatile. It is used for various functions, including feeding, drinking, grabbing objects, and communication.
    • Both male and female Indian elephants may have tusks, though those of males are generally larger. Tusks are elongated, modified incisor teeth.
    • Some individuals may not have visible tusks.
    • The skin of Indian elephants is generally grayish, with patches of pinkish skin on the ears, face, and trunk. The skin is rough and can be wrinkled.
    • They are known for their intelligence and are considered one of the most intelligent mammal species.
    • Indian elephants have a large and highly developed neocortex, the part of the brain responsible for complex cognitive processes such as problem-solving, learning, and memory.
    Endemic purple-faced Langur
    • Their diet primarily consists of a variety of plant materials.
    • Here are some details about the diet of the purple-faced langur:
    1. Leaves
    2. Fruits
    3. Flowers and Buds
    4. Seeds
    5. Burk and Gum
  • As the name suggests, the most prominent feature of the purple-faced langur is its distinctive purple face.
  • The fur on the face ranges from dark purple to pinkish hues, giving these monkeys a unique and striking appearance.
  • Apart from the purple face, the rest of the body is covered in grayish or dark brown fur.
  • The fur is generally short and dense.
  • Their eyes are relatively large, providing good vision, which is important for navigating through the forest canopy.
  • Their tail is longer than their body and is often used for balance as they move through trees
  • Their hands and feet have grasping digits, which help them maneuver through the trees with ease.
