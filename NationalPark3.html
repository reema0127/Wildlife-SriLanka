Wild Life Sites in Sri Lanka

  • Kumana National Park

    • Kumana National Park, situated in Sri Lanka, has earned acclaim for its remarkable avifauna, notably hosting extensive gatherings of migratory waterfowl and wading birds. Positioned 391 kilometers (243 mi) southeast of Colombo along the southeastern coast of Sri Lanka, Kumana seamlessly connects with Yala National Park. Originally named Yala East National Park, it officially adopted the name Kumana on 5 September 2006.

    The park faced a period of closure from 1985 to March 2003 due to attacks by the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE). Additionally, Kumana National Park was impacted by the devastating Boxing Day tsunami in 2004. Despite these challenges, the park continues to be a haven for diverse bird species, drawing nature enthusiasts and birdwatchers to witness the captivating avian life in this unique ecosystem.

    Established in 1938, the Kumana Bird Sanctuary is an integral part of the Kumana National Park in Sri Lanka. Renowned as a vital nesting and breeding ground, this sanctuary boasts a rich avian population with a recorded count of 255 bird species. Notably, between April and July, the Kumana swamp area witnesses a spectacular migration of tens of thousands of birds.

    Among the breeding inhabitants, rare species such as the black-necked stork, lesser adjutant, Eurasian spoonbill, and great thick-knee, belonging to the families Scolopacidae and Charadriidae, are prominent. The swamp attracts migratory visitors like pintail snipes, covering distances of 9,000 to 11,000 kilometers from Siberia. Large flocks of Asian openbill, glossy ibis, purple heron, great egret, Indian pond heron, black-crowned night heron, intermediate egret, little egret, spot-billed pelican, Indian cormorant, little cormorant, common moorhen, watercock,lesser whistling duck, and little grebe contribute to the vibrant birdlife.

    Noteworthy among the migratory rarities are the yellow-footed green pigeon, greater racket-tailed drongo, Malabar trogon, red-faced malkoha, and sirkeer malkoha. Additionally, common wading birds like the Pacific golden plover, greater sand plover, lesser sand plover, grey plover, ruddy turnstone, little ringed plover, wood sandpiper, marsh sandpiper, common redshank, common sandpiper, curlew sandpiper, little stint, common snipe, and pintail snipe add to the biodiversity.

    The Kumana region also supports a variety of aquatic life, with tilapia and mullet being the primary fish varieties, occasionally accompanied by Channa spp. The park is home to reptiles such as the mugger crocodile, Indian flap-shelled turtle, and Indian black turtle. Sri Lankan Pelicans
  • Pelicans in Sri Lanka, like their counterparts elsewhere, have a diverse diet that primarily consists of fish.

  • Sri Lankan pelicans are large birds with a wingspan ranging from about 8 to 10 feet (2.5 to 3 meters).
  • They typically have a predominantly white plumage, with black markings on their wings and tail.
  • The head and neck may have a yellowish tint, and during the breeding season, adult birds may develop a pinkish hue on the upper chest
  • Pelicans are known for their long bills with a distinctive pouch
  • Pelicans have relatively small eyes that are often surrounded by bare skin.
  • Pelicans are known for their distinctive, expandable throat pouches.
  • Pelicans are highly adaptable and can be found in various habitats, including coastal areas, lakes, and rivers.
  • Pelicans primarily feed on fish, using their long bills and pouches to scoop up prey
  • They are known for their unique hunting technique, where they float on the water's surface and use their bills to catch fish.
  • Pelicans are known for their characteristic gliding flight, often flying in V-shaped formations.

    Little Cormorants
    • Little Cormorants (Microcarbo niger) are aquatic birds that primarily feed on fish but also consume a variety of other aquatic prey.
    • Here are some details about the diet of Little Cormorants:
      1. Fish
      2. Crustaceans
      3. Insects
      4. Amphibians
      5. Diet Variation
    • As the name suggests, the Little Cormorant is relatively small compared to some other cormorant species.
    • It typically measures around 50 to 55 cm (20 to 22 inches) in length.
    • In breeding plumage, Little Cormorants often have a glossy, all-black plumage.
    • The bill of the Little Cormorant is relatively thin and hooked at the tip.
    • Little Cormorants have a long and slender neck, which they use for striking underwater to catch prey.
    • Their legs are short, and their feet are webbed, which aids in swimming.
    • Little Cormorants are widespread and can be found in a variety of aquatic habitats, including lakes, rivers, ponds, and coastal areas.
    • Their diet primarily consists of fish, but they may also consume other aquatic prey, such as crustaceans and insects
    • Little Cormorants are known for their guttural and croaking calls.
    • Little Cormorants can be seen perched with their wings spread wide to dry their feathers
